explores the applications of remotely sensed data in policy-making. The presentation consists of 64 slides and covers various topics such as the use of different sensors to obtain data, the interpretation of different wavelengths, and the different applications of remote sensing in environmental studies.

The lecture begins with an introduction on how to use the presentation and proceeds to outline the lecture. The first part focuses on example applications, including multi-temporal land cover mapping, forest fire monitoring, pollution monitoring, change detection, drought indices, vegetation stress, informal housing detection, precipitation, water level data monitoring, elevation models, building or network outline extraction, temperature monitoring, and night time lights.

The second part of the lecture deals with policy challenges and their relation to remote sensing data. Topics covered include air pollution and land use/land cover, urban green spaces, disaster response/preparedness, and synthetic aperture radar (SAR). The presentation also discusses the different sensors used for SAR, the various wavelengths and polarizations used, and the comparisons between SAR and other technologies.

Overall, the lecture emphasizes the significance of remotely sensed data in providing answers to policy-related questions, with applications ranging from environmental monitoring to disaster preparedness.
